For anyone exploring Chinese incense, understanding agarwood quality is essential. With prices ranging from a few dollars per gram for basic material to tens of thousands for exceptional wild pieces, knowing what distinguishes one grade from another is both a practical skill and a window into the depth of incense culture.

## The Traditional Grading System

Chinese tradition classifies agarwood primarily by density, which directly correlates with resin content. The traditional system recognizes four main grades:

### Grade 1: Sinking Grade (沉水)
The highest grade. A piece of Grade 1 agarwood sinks immediately when placed in water. The wood is so saturated with resin that its density exceeds 1.0 g/cm3. Characteristics:

- Color: Deep black or dark chocolate brown throughout
- Texture: Waxy, dense, with no visible pale wood fibers
- Fragrance: Intense, complex, with exceptional longevity (6-8 hours on a heater)
- Price: Hundreds to thousands of dollars per gram for exceptional pieces

True sinking-grade agarwood is extraordinarily rare. A piece the size of a thumb can cost more than a luxury watch.

### Grade 2: Hovering Grade (沉水浮)
These pieces hover in water - they sink slowly when pushed down but would not sink on their own. Density is close to 1.0 g/cm3 but slightly below.

- Color: Dark brown with occasional lighter streaks
- Texture: Mostly resinous but visible wood grain structure
- Fragrance: Rich and complex, 4-6 hours of burn time
- Price: Tens to hundreds of dollars per gram

### Grade 3: Floating Grade (浮水)
Floats readily on water. The wood is partially infused with resin but retains significant wood fiber.

- Color: Light to medium brown with clearly visible grain
- Texture: More wood than resin, somewhat fibrous
- Fragrance: Pleasant but less intense, 2-4 hours duration
- Price: A few dollars to several tens of dollars per gram

### Grade 4: Lightly Infused
Minimal resin content. The wood retains most of its natural pale appearance with small dark streaks or spots.

- Color: Pale beige or light tan with minor dark areas
- Texture: Lightweight, fibrous, wood-like
- Fragrance: Subtle, suitable for daily use or blending
- Price: Under 10 dollars per gram

## The Water Test

The water test is the most practical way to assess agarwood density at home. Use clean, room-temperature water and observe how the piece behaves:

1. **Sinks immediately** - Grade 1 potential (but confirm by checking that the dark color runs through the entire piece, not just the surface)
2. **Sinks slowly, hovers** - Grade 2
3. **Floats but sits low in the water** - Grade 3
4. **Floats high** - Grade 4 or cultivated material

A word of caution: some sellers apply surface treatments to make lower-grade material appear denser. Always examine a fresh break or cut to confirm the resin penetration.

## Beyond Density: Fragrance Quality

While density is the traditional metric, experienced connoisseurs know that fragrance quality does not perfectly correlate with density. A Grade 3 piece from an exceptional source can be more satisfying than a Grade 1 piece with a harsh or one-dimensional profile.

Evaluate agarwood fragrance on these criteria:

**Complexity**: Does the scent evolve over time? A great piece reveals distinct top, heart, and base notes. A simple piece smells the same from start to finish.

**Clarity**: Is the fragrance clean, or is there a smoky, burnt, or sour undertone? Clean material suggests proper harvesting and storage.

**Persistence**: How long does the fragrance last on the heater? Premium material can scent a room for 6-8 hours from a single piece the size of a grain of rice.

**Character**: Does the fragrance feel "alive" - dynamic and responsive to small temperature changes? The finest agarwood shifts noticeably between 160°C and 200°C.

## Regional Characteristics

Agarwood's character varies significantly by origin:

**Hainan, China**: Considered by many the finest. Exceptionally complex, with a cool, floral opening and deep, sweet base. Very rare - most Hainan trees were harvested decades ago.

**Vietnam**: The classic profile. Rich, sweet, and balsamic with excellent complexity. Vietnamese agarwood was the standard of quality for centuries of Chinese incense culture.

**Cambodia**: Often described as "sweet" and "clean" with pronounced vanilla-like notes. Usually less dense than Vietnamese material but highly fragrant.

**Indonesia (Papua, Kalimantan)**: Generally darker, heavier, and more resinous. The fragrance is earthier and more pungent - prized in the Middle Eastern market and increasingly appreciated in China.

**India**: Wild Indian agarwood is exceptionally rare today due to protection laws. Historical accounts describe it as intensely sweet with a unique spicy undertone.

## Cultivated vs. Wild Agarwood

Most agarwood on the market today is cultivated. This is not necessarily a negative - good cultivated material can be excellent. The key differences:

| Attribute | Wild Agarwood | Cultivated Agarwood |
|---|---|---|
| Formation | Natural infection, 30+ years | Inoculated, 5-10 years |
| Density | Often higher | Lighter, more variable |
| Complexity | Greater depth and evolution | Simpler, more linear |
| Price | Very high | Accessible |
| Sustainability | Questionable | High |

For daily practice, quality cultivated agarwood from reputable producers offers excellent value. For special sessions and deep study, wild pieces (when ethically sourced) provide an unmatched experience.

## Practical Buying Tips

1. **Buy from reputable dealers** who specialize in incense materials, not general Asian grocery suppliers
2. **Start with small quantities** - 5-10 grams is enough to learn
3. **Keep a journal** of each piece you try, noting density, fragrance character, and actual burning time
4. **Compare side by side** - heat two different grades at the same time to train your nose
5. **Trust your own perception** - the best agarwood is the one that speaks to you, not the one with the highest price tag

Agarwood is a lifetime study. Even experienced collectors continue to discover new nuances in materials they have known for years. The journey of learning to discern quality is itself one of the great pleasures of Chinese incense practice.
